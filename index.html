Test-Time Training for Modality Order Consistency in Vision-Language Models

Aditi Gupta, Yossi Gandelsman
TTIC / University of Chicago
Paper (coming soon) Code

The image and the question are identical in both prompts. Only their order changes — and the model's answer changes with it.

Abstract

Vision-language models are typically evaluated with the image placed before the question, but nothing about the task requires that order. We show that simply swapping the two — presenting the question before the image — produces a large and consistent drop in accuracy across models and benchmarks, despite the two prompts being semantically identical.

Using activation patching, we localize this failure to a narrow band of mid-network layers. Motivated by this, we introduce a KL-aligned test-time training objective that adapts a small set of mid-network layers to align the weaker ordering with the stronger one, requiring no retraining, no labels, and no architectural changes.

A consistent gap across models and benchmarks

Presenting the question before the image lowers accuracy on every model and benchmark we tested — from 5 to 26 accuracy points, across InternVL2, Qwen2.5-VL, and Qwen3-VL.

The gap is largest on MMStar (+26.1 points for InternVL2), the benchmark that depends most heavily on the image, and smallest on RealWorldQA for the newer models (+5.2 points for Qwen3-VL). Newer models shrink the gap but do not remove it.

The failure is localized to a narrow mid-network band

To find where the two orderings diverge, we patch image-first hidden states into the question-first forward pass one layer at a time and measure whether the answer flips to the correct one.

Patching has almost no effect through the first fifteen layers. Between layers 15 and 19 accuracy rises sharply, then plateaus. Restricting attention to the examples that matter — those the model gets right image-first and wrong question-first — makes the effect starker: recovery on this subset jumps to roughly 80–93% across the same narrow band.

The failure is carried by MLP sublayers

Splitting the network into attention heads and MLP sublayers shows the two behave very differently under a change of modality order.

Before adaptation the MLP sublayers are strongly anti-aligned across the two orderings (NIoU −0.82), while attention heads are only weakly aligned either way (+0.10 before, +0.15 after). Test-time training flips the MLP misalignment to strong agreement (−0.82 → +0.84), and does so regardless of which layer window is adapted.

Qualitative examples

Individual examples show the two orderings converging over test-time training steps: the question-first trajectory moves onto the correct answer while the image-first trajectory holds.
